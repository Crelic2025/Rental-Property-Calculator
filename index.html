Quick Screens
60-second sanity checks used by experienced investors
These are rough filters to quickly eliminate bad deals. The 1% Rule checks if rent is at least 1% of the price (hard to hit in 2026 — 0.7%+ is realistic). The 50% Rule assumes half your rent goes to expenses and checks if the rest covers the mortgage. GRM tells you how many years of rent it takes to equal the price — lower is better.
A/B
Live-In vs. Move-Out
The deal must work in both scenarios
This is the most important test. Scenario A = you live there, tenants offset your mortgage. Scenario B = you've moved out, all units rented. If Scenario B is negative, reject the deal — no exceptions. You'll eventually move out, and the property needs to stand on its own.

Return Metrics & FHA Test
Is the deal earning a good return on your money?
Cap Rate = what you'd earn if you bought with all cash (no mortgage). Useful for comparing properties. Cash-on-Cash = the actual return on the money you put in — compare this to a savings account to see if the risk is worth it. DSCR = does the property's income cover the mortgage? Lenders want 1.25x+. FHA Test = required for 3–4 unit FHA loans.
🔥
Stress Test
Does the deal survive a bad year?
This simulates a recession: rents drop 10% AND expenses rise 15% at the same time. If the deal goes negative under stress, it's an automatic reject. Every investor has a bad year eventually — this test makes sure yours doesn't bankrupt you.
⚖
Sensitivity Matrix
How fragile is this deal? Cash flow across rent × rate combos
This grid shows monthly cash flow (Scenario B) if rents come in lower than you expected AND rates were higher. Your current inputs are outlined in blue. Green cells pass. Red cells lose money. If half the grid is red, the deal has no margin for error.
💰
Can You Afford It?
Where your cash goes + do you have enough reserves?
Your cash gets split into 4 buckets: down payment, closing costs, repairs, and an emergency reserve. You must keep at least 3 months of (mortgage + living expenses) in reserve after closing. If there's not enough left, this deal requires more cash than you have.
$
What Should You Actually Offer?
Maximum Allowable Offer — your negotiation anchor
This is the single most useful number in the calculator. The MAO is the highest price where every test passes. It doesn't care what the seller is asking — it only cares about the math. If the asking price is above the MAO, that gap is exactly how much you need to negotiate down. Bring this number to your agent.
▸ Advanced: Set minimum return targets
These are optional. By default the MAO finds the price where you break even. Setting targets here makes the MAO stricter — it will only show a price where you also hit your return goals. Try 0 first, then bump these up to see how much it tightens.
$0 = break even. $200 = require $200/mo surplus.
